Renascent

A third-person action RPG set at the threshold between life and the underworld. Die. Return. Ascend.

Late December 2025. I had just finished the Dark Souls trilogy for the first time — back to back, like a normal person — and somewhere between the end credits of DS3 and staring at the ceiling at 2am, I thought: what if I made this type of thing, but set it in my world.

I've always been more of a multiplayer guy. Competitive shooters with friends, that kind of thing. Solo games were always something I was interested in but could never sit down and actually get through. Dark Souls was different. DS1 had me hooked almost immediately, and by the time I finished DS3 I was a changed man. So naturally, I decided to make a game.

Obviously I wasn't going to build a full Dark Souls campaign — I'm one person, I have a job, I'm in university, let's be realistic. So I started thinking about formats. What kind of game could give a lot of playtime without requiring an unreasonable amount of content creation? The answer came from an unexpected place: a roguelite dungeon buried inside Final Fantasy XIV. As a format, it's a clever thing — replayable, self-contained, with enough structure to feel like a real game without needing a sixty-hour campaign's worth of assets.

That was the spark. Take that format, dress it in souls-like combat, wrap it in an original fantasy world drawn from northern pagan mythology and folklore, and see what happens.

The world

You are dead. That's where the story starts.

The player finds themselves at the threshold of the afterlife — soul flickering, the doors to eternal rest within reach. But there are forces stuck in the space between worlds. Ancient spirits, long-imprisoned, who can't move on without help. They ask you to return. To go back amongst the living, climb the impossible tower they've constructed, and prove yourself worthy. In doing so, you set them free.

The world you play through is their creation — a kind of alternative reality built as a testing ground. It feels familiar in some ways, strange and enchanted in others. The deeper you go, the more the story reveals itself — not through cutscenes or exposition dumps, but through passing conversations, item descriptions, and runes carved into walls you might walk past without looking twice.
